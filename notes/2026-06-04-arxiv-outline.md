We evaluate on the two SOPs that exercise different conversation shapes (insurance renewal, medical scheduling) and note credit_card as future work.
2. **Empirical predictor is the workhorse — not MCTS.** Architecture section leads with the empirical predictor (lookups over accumulated precedent traces, 88% of pool fetches in our measurements). Background MCTS pondering is described as architectural support that is wired-in and verified, but its contribution under our test conditions is small (3%). The paper does *not* claim MCTS performance.
3. **"Honest prompts" is one of three contributions, not the headline.** Title and lead emphasize the architectural delivery (pool reframe + cosine-only rerank). The honest-prompts result is presented as the third contribution with its full ablation, but the paper is not restructured around it.

## One-sentence summary

A two-system architecture (live agent + asynchronous supervisor mediated by a blackboard) for SOP-constrained voice agents, with three concrete contributions: a pool-based speculative cache that decisively beats key-lookup, a cosine-only rerank that removes the LLM from the critical path without quality loss, and a principle for prompt design in async systems ("honest prompts").

## Section structure

### 1. Introduction (~0.75 pages)

- The voice-agent latency–intelligence trade-off (15s silence vs incompetent fast model)
- Standard middle-ground responses don't scale to regulated multi-step conversations
- We reframe as a two-system problem (slow brain / fast mouth)
- Three concrete contributions:
  1. **Speculatively-populated pool cache.** Drives effective hit rate from 11% (an exact-key lookup over the same prediction stream, our internal baseline) to 96%, by replacing exact-match key lookup with per-turn supervisor curation over a session-scoped knowledge pool. (Framing: the novelty is in the *speculative population policy* — rollout-driven prefetch — not in the pool data structure, which is a vector store at heart.)
  2. **Cosine-only rerank with explicit dedup.** Removes the per-turn LLM rerank call entirely. p95 rerank latency drops from 2441 ms to 483 ms across N=10 in two SOPs (zero quality regression once paired with contribution 3).
  3. **Speculative-context prompt.** Quality regression from (2) recovered by a prompt rewrite that makes the response-gen prompt explicitly acknowledge that prefetched items are speculation and may be off-topic. Architecturally simpler than a cosine-threshold floor and more transferable: any async-cache consumer benefits from a prompt whose claim matches what the architecture actually guarantees.

### 2. Background (~0.75 pages)

- AsyncMLD (arXiv 2312.13925) — async actor/planner pattern from robotics, with shared blackboard. Cite and credit.
- PCA-M (arXiv 2407.03884) — MCTS-over-SOPs for dialogue planning. Cite as planning machinery foundation.
- Speculative execution in distributed systems (general background)
- Cache-augmented and RAG-augmented dialogue systems (related-work positioning)

### 3. Architecture (~1.5 pages)

**Figure**: Two-system block diagram (port from blog).
**Figure**: SOP graph with state transitions, data-dep badges, happy path overlay (port from blog).

- The live agent and the supervisor, separated by a blackboard
- SOP structure: agent actions, user states, cohorts × moods
- **The supervisor's predictor stack (in order of contribution in our measurements):**
  - **Empirical predictor (workhorse).** A lookup over accumulated `precedent_traces` — historical (cohort, state, action) tuples from past sessions, with empirical transition frequencies. Cheap, deterministic, scales with deployment data. Provides the bulk (88%) of pool fetches.
  - **Live planning residuals.** Residual prefetch hints generated by the per-turn router and action proposer; 9% of pool fetches.
  - **Background MCTS pondering.** Depth-K rollouts over predicted future states (inherited from PCA-M). Architecturally supported and verified to route correctly via the prefetch manager, but contributes only 3% of fetches in our test conditions because most pondering runs are cancelled by the next turn before they complete (see §7.1 and Limitations). Pondering is *production-leveraged* rather than load-bearing in this paper's measurements.
- The pool (session knowledge store): per-session bag of `{payload, summary, source_tags, summary_embedding, TTL}`
- Query-aware data prefetch: rollout's predicted user text drives parameterised RAG queries

Framing for reviewers: this paper validates the *architecture and its two simpler predictors* (empirical + live residuals). The MCTS pondering machinery is part of the system's design and is documented for completeness, but its independent contribution awaits real-call evaluation where the inter-turn pause is long enough for pondering to finish.

### 4. Contribution 1 — The pool reframe (~1 page)

**The problem with key-lookup caches in this setting.** When the data depends on what the user *just said* (RAG queries, parameterised lookups), exact-key prediction is unreliable. We measured 60% partial overlap between predicted and live RAG queries — useful signal that key-lookup discards as a cache miss.

**The pool.** Drop the key. Append every speculatively-fetched item to a per-session pool. At each turn, rerank the pool against the live user message and attach the top-3 to the response prompt.

**Measurement (Table)**:

| | Key-lookup baseline | Pool architecture |
|---|---|---|
| Effective hit rate | 11% | 96% |

Failed predictions don't waste fetches — they remain available in the pool for future turns.

### 5. Contribution 2 — Cosine-only rerank (~1.5 pages)

**The latency story arc as a research narrative.** Three iterations, pre-committed thresholds, honest negative results.

**Iteration 1 — Input-token filter.** Hypothesis: large rerank prompts (~6-8K tokens) cause latency. Pre-filter pool to top-8 by cosine before sending to LLM. **Result: latency unchanged.** Prompt size doesn't dominate; the LLM call itself does. (Mean 1900 → 1698 ms; p95 2441 → 2452 ms.)

**Iteration 2 — Confidence-gate fast path.** Hypothesis: when embedding ranking is decisive (K-th cosine ≥ 0.50 AND gap ≥ 0.05), skip the LLM. Pre-committed adoption target: 60-80%. **Adoption result: 0%.** Why: real pool cosines cluster around 0.30 with median K-vs-(K+1) gap of 0.013 — no cliffs. The gate was unreachable.

*Figure*: cosine distribution number line (we have this) showing observed band 0.149–0.421 vs unreachable 0.50 threshold.

**Iteration 3 — Cosine-only rerank.** Cosine investigation showed 87% LLM-vs-embedding pick overlap; 77% of events have *all* LLM picks in embedding top-8. The unique value the LLM was adding was dedup, not relevance selection. So: skip the LLM entirely, take top-3 by cosine after a (dependency, summary-prefix) + (pairwise-cosine ≥ 0.95) dedup pass.

**Measurement (Table)** across 3 SOPs, N=5 per SOP:

| | Pre-fix | Input filter | Confidence gate | Cosine + dedup |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Mean rerank | ~1900 ms | 1698 ms | 1523 ms | **300 ms** |
| p95 rerank | 2441 ms | 2452 ms | 2167 ms | **483 ms** |
| Effective hit rate | 96% | 99% | 95% | **100%** |
| Target p95 < 800 ms | ✗ | ✗ | ✗ | **✓** |

### 6. Contribution 3 — The speculative-context prompt (~1 page)

**A subtle quality regression.** After shipping the cosine-only rerank, success rate dropped 5/5 → 3/5 on car_insurance. Failure mode: closing-loop. On user turns like "Thanks, I'll review the options and get back to you", top pool cosine fell to 0.10-0.18 — nothing in the pool actually about what the user said. The cosine rerank still picked 3 items. The agent dutifully responded *about those items* instead of closing the call.

**The pick-count distribution diagnostic**:

| Picks per call | LLM rerank | Cosine + dedup (no LLM) |
|---|---|---|
| 0 | 4% | 0% |
| 1-2 | 25% | 9% |
| 3+ | 71% | 91% |

The LLM had been picking zero when the pool didn't fit. Its prompt said so: *"Picking nothing is fine if the pool doesn't fit the situation."* The cosine rerank had no such escape.

**The instinctive fix and why it was wrong.** Add a cosine floor: if top-cos < 0.20, return zero picks. Would have worked, but is a brittle heuristic, and threshold tuning is the wrong shape of intervention for the actual problem.

**The actual problem.** The response-gen prompt's label read `PREFETCHED CONTEXT (curated by supervisor — high relevance to current turn)`. It made a claim that was true under LLM-curation but became false under cosine-only ranking. The architecture didn't change, but the prompt stopped being honest about what the architecture guaranteed.

**The fix.** Rewrite the label to match the architecture's actual semantics:

```
PREFETCHED CONTEXT (speculatively pre-staged — may or may not fit this turn):
The supervisor pre-fetched these items based on predictions about what the user
might ask. They may help your reply, or they may be off-topic — especially when
the user is wrapping up, making small talk, or asking about something unanticipated.
Evaluate each item against what the user just said. Use what is relevant; ignore
what isn't. If nothing fits the current message, reply naturally as if no context
was provided — do NOT force irrelevant data into the response.
```

**Measurement**:

| | Curated framing | Speculative framing |
|---|---|---|
| Success rate | 3/5 | 4/5 + 1 cap-hit |
| Mean rerank latency | 378 ms | 320 ms |
| p95 rerank latency | 940 ms | 429 ms |

**Principle** (the transferable result): *In an asynchronous AI architecture where one component speculates and another consumes, the prompt that conveys the speculation to the consumer must reflect the speculation's uncertainty. A prompt that overstates confidence — claiming relevance the architecture cannot guarantee — degrades downstream quality more than a brittle threshold could compensate for.*

| SOP | Success | Mean | p95 | MCTS on path |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| car_insurance_renewal | 5/5 | 293 ms | 490 ms | 0/60 |
| medical_appointment_booking | 5/5 | 304 ms | 453 ms | 0/60 |

The two SOPs exercise different conversation shapes — outbound sales (car insurance) vs inbound triage (medical scheduling) — and the architecture's latency profile is essentially identical on both, which is the property we wanted to verify.

A third SOP (credit_card_activation) is in the codebase but is excluded from this paper's evaluation because it exhibits a known action-proposer issue (the agent loops on `HandleObjection` ↔ `Objecting`) that lives upstream of the rerank work. Listed as future work in §9.

### 7.1 What's actually populating the pool

The supervisor's predictor stack contributes to the pool in proportions that matter for interpreting the rest of the paper:

| Predictor source | Fetches contributed |
|---|---|
| Empirical (precedent traces) | 85 (88%) |
| Live planning residuals | 9 (9%) |
| Background MCTS pondering | 3 (3%) |

**The empirical predictor is the load-bearing component in our measurements.** The headline latency and quality numbers in §7 are achieved by the empirical predictor populating the pool with high accuracy, the cosine-only rerank curating it cheaply, and the speculative-context prompt teaching the agent to ignore misfits — without depending on MCTS pondering.

**Why pondering under-contributes here.** Of 213 pondering runs spawned across the 10 sessions, 209 (98%) were cancelled before completing because the next user turn arrived before pondering finished. Pondering's average duration was 7.4 s; the autopilot's average turn cycle was 6.0 s with zero pause between turns. This is a worst-case condition for pondering: real voice calls have 2-5 s user pauses plus 1-2 s of response generation, giving pondering ~6-8 s to finish — meaningfully closer to its 7.4 s requirement. Pondering's consume rate in real-call conditions is an explicit follow-up.

The architectural framing for reviewers: the system is *designed* to use MCTS pondering and the wiring is verified, but the paper's contributions stand on the empirical predictor and the rerank/prompt design. We don't claim MCTS performance in this paper.

### 8. Discussion (~0.75 pages)

**When the pool reframe works.** Architectures where prefetch is driven by predictions that are *approximately* right (60% partial overlap). The pool gives the system another chance to use the data; key-lookup throws it away.

**When the cosine-only rerank works.** When the candidates in the pool are roughly aligned with the user's turn space (which our cosine investigation showed — 87% LLM/embedding overlap). For pools where items are highly heterogeneous (different domains, different intents), the LLM's discrimination may still add value.

**When the speculative-context prompt principle applies.** Any system where downstream consumers read from a speculatively-populated surface: blackboards, vector stores, search caches. The principle is to make the prompt's claim match the architecture's actual guarantee.

### 9. Limitations (explicit) (~0.5 pages)

- **N=5 per cell.** Not statistically convincing for fine-grained claims. We use Pass/Fail at pre-committed thresholds rather than statistical inference; N=5 is for coarse-grain falsification, not effect-size estimation. Confidence intervals on success-rate proportions (Wilson 95%) should be reported in the final paper.
- **Smart simulator, not real users.** External-validity gap. Some claims will need real-call data to carry weight.
- **Two SOPs evaluated.** car_insurance_renewal (outbound sales) and medical_appointment_booking (inbound triage). A third (credit_card_activation) is in the codebase but excluded due to an unrelated action-proposer issue; investigating that issue is future work that would broaden the evaluation.
- **Instruction-prefetch not measured.** The architecture supports it; we haven't validated the lookahead-quality assumption.
- **OpenAI embedding RTT.** Our p95 latency includes one embedding API call per rerank; production deployments could batch or cache to lower this.
- **MCTS pondering under-test.** Autopilot conditions cancel 98% of background MCTS pondering before completion (no pause between turns). The architectural pieces are in place and verified to route correctly, but our measurements do not exercise pondering's contribution. Real-call conditions, where the inter-turn pause exceeds pondering's avg duration, would give a different picture and are an explicit follow-up. **Consequently, this paper does not claim MCTS pondering as a contribution** — the empirical predictor plus the rerank/prompt design carry the headline numbers.
- **No comparison to published cache baselines.** Our "key-lookup vs pool" comparison contrasts our own architectural variants. A rigorous prior-art comparison (e.g., against GPTCache, semantic-RAG baselines, or session-scoped vector retrieval) is future work — we expect the pool's advantage to come from speculative population, not the cache shape, and that hypothesis needs direct testing.
- **The "honest prompts" principle rests on one ablation in one system.** Generalising beyond our setting will require ablations in other speculative-cache architectures.

### 10. Related work (~0.75 pages — expanded from initial outline)

Group citations along four threads:

**Async actor/planner architectures.** AsyncMLD (Sun et al., 2023 — arXiv 2312.13925) is the direct ancestor: a fast low-level controller acts in real time while a slow high-level planner refines the plan async via a shared blackboard. Originally a robotics pattern; we apply it to SOP-constrained voice agents. Also: dual-process AI architectures (Kahneman-system-1/2 framings in recent LLM work), agent supervision literature.

**Planning-based dialogue.** PCA-M (Hu et al., 2024 — arXiv 2407.03884) — MCTS-over-SOPs for action selection. Our planner machinery is built on theirs; our delta is moving the planner off the critical path. Related: state-machine dialogue control (multiple), constraint-based dialogue policies.

**Cache-augmented generation and retrieval-augmented dialogue.** CAG / KV-cache reuse (recent work on caching the LLM KV state for repeated context). GPTCache and semantic-cache literature. RAG variants where retrieval happens per-turn vs per-session. Our positioning: the pool is closer to session-scoped RAG than to KV-cache; we differ in that population is *speculative* (predicted, not user-initiated), and the rerank is done by the supervisor rather than top-K cosine alone.

**Speculative execution in LLM serving.** Speculative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023), speculative prefilling, draft-model speculation. Our use of "speculative" is at a different layer (data prefetch and instruction pre-staging, not token-level decoding) but the conceptual heritage is the same: bet on a likely future, do work for it ahead of time, fall back gracefully when wrong.

**Latency-aware LLM serving.** Recent systems work on response-time-bounded LLM deployment. Our contribution is architecture-side (move slow work off the critical path) rather than serving-side (speed up the LLM call itself); the two are complementary.

**Prompt-honesty / metacognition prompting.** Recent work asking LLMs to surface uncertainty, refuse out-of-distribution requests, or reason about confidence. Our "speculative-context prompt" is in this family but specifically at the *consumer* side of an async pipeline — the principle is that the prompt should reflect the architecture's actual guarantee, which generalises beyond LLM uncertainty to any speculative system.

Target: ~15-20 citations spread across these threads. Heavy on the first two (anchor citations) and CAG/RAG (closest comparators).

### 11. Conclusion (~0.4 pages — expanded)

We presented a two-system architecture for SOP-constrained voice agents with three measured contributions:
1. A speculatively-populated pool cache that drives effective hit rate from 11% to 96% over our internal baseline by abandoning exact-key matching.
2. A cosine-only rerank with explicit dedup that removes the LLM from the per-turn critical path, dropping p95 latency from 2441 ms to 483 ms.
3. A "speculative-context" prompt principle: when the prompt's claim about prefetched data matches what the architecture actually guarantees (speculation, not curation), downstream quality is preserved — without needing a brittle threshold.

The most transferable result is (3). Any asynchronous AI architecture where one component speculates and another consumes faces the same question: *does the consumer's prompt match the speculation's actual reliability?* We document one case where the mismatch produced a measurable quality regression and the fix was a prompt rewrite rather than additional machinery.

The architecture's MCTS pondering is part of the design but under-tested in our autopilot conditions; real-call evaluation is the obvious next step. Instruction prefetch (pre-staging the agent's response text for predicted future turns) is supported by the architecture but unmeasured in this paper.

Code, data, and the full set of measurements are released with this preprint.
